Health Analytics Dashboard

Interactive analytics exploring Google search trends for common health issues in the United States (2004-2017)

How has the search interest for top health issues or diseases change over time? And how does it compare with the real-life leading causes of death? To investigate this question, our team used the online search data in the US from 2004 to 2017 provided by Google Trends data as well as the leading death-causing diseases data provided by the US CDC data. Google Trends data in particular allows us to see what people are searching for at a very local level. We will compare them with the actual location of occurrences for those same health conditions to understand how online search data reflects real life for millions of Americans.

Health Search Volume by State and Region

This interactive map shows health search volume across all US states from 2004 to 2017. States are color-coded based on total search volume, with darker colors indicating higher search activity. California, Texas, and New York show the highest search volumes.

Zooming in further, geologically, we will see which states or cities had more interests in searching for health issues. From 2004 to 2017, the data showed that search interest gradually increased over the years. Certain regions show a more significant increase in search interest than others. The increase in search activity was greatest in California, Texas, and New York.

The Trend of The Change in The Total Search Volume

Line chart showing overall health search trends from 2004 to 2017. Search volume shows a consistent upward trend over the 14-year period, with significant increases in later years indicating growing public interest in online health information.

It could be observed that the online searching volume of the diseases overall showed an increasing tendency. This result coincides and could be explained by technological advancements in terms of the growth of internet and people's growing reliance on telehealth.

Boxplot of Health Google Search 2004-2017

This boxplot allows us to see the amount variability in the search score (Google's own metric) in health searches from 2004 to 2017. It is interesting there a wide range for both diabetes and depression.

Boxplot visualization showing the distribution of search scores for 9 health conditions over 14 years. Each box represents the interquartile range, with whiskers showing the full range of data. Diabetes and depression show the widest variability in search scores.

Searching Trends of Each Health Condition

How does the online searching interest in each health condition change in themselves per se? All of the observed diseases had demonstrated an upward tendency over the years from 2004 to 2017.This multi line graph presents a dramatic illustration of how the public is increasingly obtaining health care information by searching online.

Multi-line chart showing individual search trends for 9 health conditions over time. All conditions show upward trends, with cancer consistently having the highest search volume. Lines can be toggled on/off for focused comparison.

Often, many diseases have comorbidities; that is, they tend to co-occur with other diseases. For example, it could be assumed that obesity and diabetes might be largely correlated, and our data confirms that assumption. (Hint: You could click on the health conditions to highlight only obesity and diabetes to compare them.)

But more comprehensively, how do all the health conditions correlate with each other?

Health Search in Each State

The detailed searching trends of each disease in each state are displayed below.

Grouped bar chart comparing search volumes across different states for 9 health conditions. Each state has 9 colored bars representing different health conditions. Cancer consistently shows the highest search volume across most states.

Apart from the main effects where California, Texas, and New York had the largest overall online search volumes, it is shown that cancer had been overwhelmingly the most searched health condition among all in most of the states.

Total Volume of Health Search

The following visualization confirms that cancer's popularity in online search. Interestingly, the frequency of searches does not necessarily correlate with the frequency of the condition in the general population, as searches for stroke information are more common than cardiovascular searches even though strokes are more infrequent than cardiovascular disease.

Radar chart (spider chart) showing comparative search volumes for 9 health conditions. Cancer shows the largest area, indicating highest total search volume. Each axis represents a different health condition.

Leading Causes of Death Trends

Radar chart showing 10 leading causes of death in the US from 2004 to 2017. Diseases of the heart and malignant neoplasms (cancer) show the highest death rates. Data is normalized per 100,000 population.

Correlations Among Health Conditions

The following diagram shows the online search correlations among all the observed diseases according to our data. The stronger correlations are in the darker shades.

Heatmap showing correlation coefficients between search trends for 9 health conditions. Darker colors indicate stronger correlations. Diabetes shows strong correlations with depression and obesity. Correlation values range from 0.28 to 0.74.

Of course, however, correlation does not often imply causation, so some of the correlations might still be coincidental. Our visualization only provides a reference of information.

Overall, it is observed that the online health search interest has been and presumably will be increasing in the United States, especially in the more metropolitan areas such as New York, California, and Texas. Cancer has been the most searched disease.

10 Leading Causes of Death Per 100,000 Population from 2004-2017

Line chart showing trends for 10 leading causes of death in the US from 2004 to 2017. Diseases of the heart show the highest death rates, followed by malignant neoplasms. Most causes show decreasing or stable trends over time.
